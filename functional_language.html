💡 What is functional language? Functional language = phrases we use to DO things with words. Not grammar rules — tools. When you say "I see your point, but..." you're not just speaking — you're managing a conversation. These phrases are what fluent speakers use every day.

Giving Opinions 💭

Use these phrases to introduce your opinion clearly. The strength varies — from gentle to very direct.

← softer
neutral
stronger →
I think + clause A2
"I think closing the lid is still a good habit."
The most common and neutral way to give an opinion. Safe in any situation.
I believe + clause A2
"I believe public hygiene standards need to improve."
Slightly more formal than "I think." Suggests more personal conviction.
I feel + clause A2
"I feel that the study results are quite surprising."
More emotional than "think" or "believe." Used when the opinion is personal or value-based.
In my opinion, + clause B1
"In my opinion, the government should invest more in public health."
More formal. Often used to introduce a strong or controversial opinion. ⚠️ Don't say "In my opinion, I think..." — it's redundant.
In my view, + clause B1
"In my view, this research is very important for public health."
Interchangeable with "In my opinion." Slightly more academic-sounding.
From my perspective, + clause B1
"From my perspective, hygiene at home is more important than in public."
Implies your opinion comes from your specific experience or background.
As far as I'm concerned, + clause B1
"As far as I'm concerned, this changes nothing about my bathroom routine."
Strong and direct. Can sound dismissive if overused. Use when you want to be firm.
Personally, + clause B1
"Personally, I'm more worried about my toothbrush than the toilet lid."
Makes clear this is your own view — not a general fact. Great for discussion questions.
It seems to me that + clause B2
"It seems to me that the real issue is how often people disinfect their bathrooms."
Softer and more analytical. Suggests you've thought about it carefully but are open to other views.
If you ask me, + clause B2
"If you ask me, the whole study is just common sense dressed up in science."
Conversational and slightly informal. Often used when giving a bold or direct opinion.
⚡ Quick Practice Try answering these out loud using a different phrase each time:
"What do you think about social media?"  ·  "Is English important?"  ·  "Should people work from home?"

Agreeing ✅

Saying "yes" is easy. Saying it fluently, with the right strength, is the goal.

Exactly! / Absolutely! / Definitely! A2
"This study is really surprising." — "Exactly! I never expected that."
One-word strong agreements. Use these to show you're listening and engaged.
You're right. / That's right. A2
"We should wash our hands more." — "You're right. Most people don't do it enough."
Simple and clear. "You're right" agrees with the person; "That's right" agrees with the fact.
That's a good point. B1
"The study only tested one type of virus." — "That's a good point. It might not apply to all germs."
Acknowledges the other person's argument. More specific than "You're right." Good for debates.
I agree (with you) on that. B1
"I think hygiene at home matters more than people realise." — "I agree with you on that completely."
A direct and clear agreement. Adding "completely" or "entirely" makes it stronger.
I couldn't agree more. B1
"Public bathrooms need more funding." — "I couldn't agree more — it's a real problem."
Emphatic strong agreement. A very natural, fluent expression. Don't overuse it.
That's exactly what I think. / My thoughts exactly. B1
"The study needs more testing before we change our habits." — "My thoughts exactly."
Shows you had the same idea. "My thoughts exactly" is slightly more conversational.
I think you're absolutely right about that. B2
"The media often exaggerates health studies." — "I think you're absolutely right about that."
Fuller and warmer. Used in more formal conversations or when you want to show real engagement.
That's a fair point. I'd have to agree. B2
"Scientists sometimes oversimplify results." — "That's a fair point. I'd have to agree."
Sounds thoughtful — like you considered it before agreeing. Great for academic discussions.
⚡ Quick Practice Your partner says these. Agree using a different phrase each time:
"English is the most useful language."  ·  "Social media has changed friendships."  ·  "People spend too much time on their phones."

Disagreeing Politely ❌

Disagreeing without being rude is one of the most important conversation skills. Always acknowledge first — then push back.

🔑 The Golden Formula Acknowledge → Soften → Disagree
"I see your point, but I think..." — Notice the structure: you say something nice first, then give your view. Never start with "No, you're wrong."
I'm not sure I agree. A2
"Closing the lid definitely prevents germs." — "I'm not sure I agree — the study says it makes no difference."
Gentle and open. Doesn't shut the other person down. Good starting point.
I see your point, but... B1
"We should all close the lid." — "I see your point, but the study shows it doesn't actually help."
Classic, natural, very commonly used. Acknowledges before disagreeing. Never sounds rude.
I understand where you're coming from, but... B1
"People worry too much about germs." — "I understand where you're coming from, but some germs are genuinely dangerous."
Stronger acknowledgement than "I see your point." Shows real empathy before you disagree.
I take your point, however... B1
"This is just one small study." — "I take your point, however, it was conducted by a top university."
"However" sounds slightly more formal and confident than "but." Good in presentations.
That's an interesting view, but I think... B1
"Science always gives us the full picture." — "That's an interesting view, but I think science has many limitations too."
Very diplomatic. Compliments the other person's view before presenting yours.
Actually, I think... / Actually, I'd argue that... B1
"Public bathrooms are generally fine." — "Actually, I'd argue that most could be much cleaner."
"Actually" softens the contradiction. "I'd argue that" is more formal and confident.
With all due respect, I don't think... B2
"This research is useless." — "With all due respect, I don't think that's a fair conclusion."
Formal. Signals you're about to strongly disagree while still being polite. Don't use in casual chat.
I'm afraid I disagree. B2
"The study proves we don't need to clean bathrooms." — "I'm afraid I disagree — it only says the lid doesn't help."
"I'm afraid" makes the disagreement sound reluctant and polite. Often used in formal English.
⚡ Quick Practice Disagree politely with these statements — use a different phrase each time:
"Grammar is not important in English."  ·  "Everyone should work six days a week."  ·  "Social media is mostly harmful."

Partial Agreement 🤝

Sometimes you don't fully agree or disagree. These phrases let you show nuance — you agree with part of what was said.

You have a point, but... B1
"Cleaning more often is the only solution." — "You have a point, but it's also about the products you use."
Acknowledges something valid in the other person's argument before adding a "but."
That's true to some extent, but... B1
"The study changes everything." — "That's true to some extent, but daily habits still matter a lot."
"To some extent" = partly true, but not completely. Very useful for nuanced discussions.
I agree up to a point, but... B1
"People are too obsessed with germs." — "I agree up to a point, but some caution is definitely necessary."
Similar to "to some extent." "Up to a point" sounds slightly more formal and deliberate.
There's something in that, but... B2
"We should all become scientists." — "There's something in that — but most people are better at other things."
Conversational. Means "that idea has some truth." Sounds very natural in British English.
I can see both sides of this. On one hand... on the other hand... B2
"On one hand, the study results are clear. On the other hand, changing lifelong habits is difficult."
Perfect for balanced arguments. Very common in academic writing and formal speaking. Shows maturity of thought.
While I understand that..., I also think... B2
"While I understand that science evolves, I also think we shouldn't abandon all hygiene habits."
Sophisticated structure. Shows you can hold two ideas at the same time. Great for B2 writing.
⚡ Quick Practice Give a partial agreement to these. Don't fully agree or disagree:
"Technology has made life better."  ·  "Young people don't respect older people anymore."  ·  "Money is the most important thing in life."

Asking for Opinions ❓

A good conversation goes both ways. Use these phrases to invite others to share their views.

What do you think (about / of) + topic? A2
"What do you think about the new study on toilet hygiene?"
The most common and neutral way to ask. Works in any situation.
How do you feel about + topic? A2
"How do you feel about public toilets in your city?"
"Feel" suggests a more personal, emotional response. Good for personal topics.
What's your view on + topic? B1
"What's your view on the government's hygiene policies?"
More formal than "what do you think." Good for professional or academic discussions.
What are your thoughts on + topic? B1
"What are your thoughts on the findings of this study?"
Very natural in both spoken and written English. "Thoughts" (plural) invites a fuller answer.
Do you think (that) + clause? B1
"Do you think people will change their bathroom habits after reading this?"
Asks for a specific opinion on a specific claim. Easier to answer than open questions.
Would you say that + clause? B2
"Would you say that hygiene standards are getting better or worse?"
More indirect and formal. Often used in interviews or academic discussions.
I'd be interested to hear your take on + topic. B2
"I'd be interested to hear your take on whether this changes anything for you."
Very polite and warm. "Take" = personal perspective. Makes the other person feel valued.

Clarifying 🔄

When you're not clear, or when you want to say something in a better way. Also includes asking someone else to clarify.

When YOU want to clarify what you said
What I mean is... A2
"I don't trust the media. What I mean is, I always look for multiple sources."
Simple and direct. Use when you think you've been misunderstood.
What I'm trying to say is... B1
"What I'm trying to say is that the research method might have limitations."
Slightly more tentative — suggests you're searching for the right words.
To put it another way, ... B1
"To put it another way, closing the lid is basically useless."
Rephrases your idea more simply. Often used after a complex explanation.
In other words, ... B1
"In other words, what we believed about the toilet lid is actually a myth."
Summarises or simplifies the previous idea. Very common in presentations.
Let me rephrase that. B2
"Let me rephrase that — I'm not saying cleaning is useless, just that the lid specifically doesn't help."
Use when you've said something that came out wrong or could be misinterpreted.
When you want someone ELSE to clarify
Sorry, could you say that again? A2
"Sorry, could you say that again? I missed the last part."
Simple and polite. Always better than just saying "What?"
What do you mean by [word/phrase]? B1
"What do you mean by 'aerial transmission' exactly?"
Asks for the definition of a specific term. Shows you're engaged and listening.
Could you elaborate on that? B2
"Could you elaborate on why the study used a non-human virus?"
Formal. Asks for more detail. Common in presentations, interviews, and academic English.
Are you saying that + clause? B2
"Are you saying that we should stop cleaning our bathrooms entirely?"
Checks understanding by restating what you think they meant. Use with a rising intonation.

Adding Information ➕

Use these to build on what you've already said — or to support your argument with more evidence.

Also, ... / And also, ... A2
"The study found no difference. Also, it recommended disinfecting all surfaces."
The simplest way to add a point. Don't start every sentence with "Also" — vary it.
Not only that, but... B1
"The lid doesn't help. Not only that, but it might actually make things worse."
Emphasises that the next point is surprising or stronger than the first. Great for dramatic effect.
What's more, ... B1
"The researchers tested two bathrooms. What's more, they ran the experiment multiple times."
Adds extra information that supports or strengthens the point. Slightly formal, very natural.
On top of that, ... B1
"Public bathrooms are often underfunded. On top of that, they're cleaned less than once a day."
Adds to a negative situation or problem. Common in spoken English and debates.
In addition (to this / to that), ... B1
"In addition to this, the researchers suggested disinfecting the toilet brush as well."
More formal. Very common in written English, reports, and presentations.
Furthermore, ... / Moreover, ... B2
"The study was thorough. Furthermore, it was conducted by a leading university."
Academic / formal. "Moreover" is slightly stronger than "furthermore." Use in essays and presentations.
To make matters worse, ... B2
"The bathroom was already dirty. To make matters worse, the disinfectant had run out."
Adds a point that makes a bad situation even worse. Dramatic and conversational.

Changing Topic 🔀

Move a conversation forward naturally — back on track, or to something related.

Anyway, ... A2
"We've talked a lot about the study. Anyway, let me ask you something different."
The most casual and common. Signals you're moving on. Can also bring a conversation back on track.
Moving on, ... / Let's move on to... B1
"Moving on, I'd like to ask about your personal experience with hygiene."
Signals a deliberate shift. Common in presentations, classes, and interviews.
That reminds me, ... B1
"That reminds me, I read another article about bacteria in kitchens last week."
Natural, conversational transition. Connects the new topic to something already said.
Speaking of which, ... B1
"We've been talking about bathrooms. Speaking of which, have you seen the new smart toilet designs?"
Links the new topic to something just mentioned. Sounds fluid and natural.
On a different note, ... / On another note, ... B2
"On a different note, do you think this study will change government health policies?"
More formal way to change topic. Signals the shift clearly without being abrupt.
Getting back to what I was saying earlier, ... B2
"Getting back to what I was saying earlier, the real problem is disinfection frequency."
Returns to a previous point. Shows you're tracking the whole conversation.

✓ Correct Answer
c — "I understand where you're coming from, but I think social media also connects people."
Polite disagreement. Acknowledge first ("I understand where you're coming from"), then present your view. Option (a) is rude. Option (d) changes the subject — not a disagreement.
4You want to know what your teacher thinks about a controversial topic. Which question is most appropriate?
✓ Correct Answer
b — "I'd be interested to hear your take on this."
Asking for opinions (politely). This is warm and respectful — it signals genuine curiosity without pressure. Option (a) is too direct. Option (c) mixes up two different functions incorrectly. Option (d) is for clarification, not asking opinions.
5You've been talking about the toilet study for a while. You want to change the subject to bathroom design. What do you say?
✓ Correct Answer
c — "Speaking of which, have you seen the new Japanese toilet designs?"
Changing topic. "Speaking of which" links the new topic (bathroom design) to the current one (bathrooms) naturally. The other options are for disagreeing and clarifying — completely wrong functions here.
6You've made a strong point. You want to add one more argument that makes it even stronger. What do you say?
✓ Correct Answer
b — "Not only that, but the study also found bacteria on the toilet brush."
Adding information. "Not only that, but..." signals a second, often stronger point. It's perfect for building an argument. Option (a) is agreement. Option (c) is clarifying. Option (d) is partial agreement.
7Which phrase is NOT appropriate for giving an opinion?
✓ Correct Answer
b — "Could you elaborate on that?"
This is a clarifying phrase — asking someone else to explain more. It's not for giving opinions. All the others (in my view / personally / if you ask me) are opinion phrases.
8Lena says something you don't fully understand. You want her to say it differently. What do you say?
✓ Correct Answer
b — "What do you mean by that exactly?"
Asking for clarification. This asks Lena to explain her specific point more clearly. Option (a) agrees without understanding — a common but bad habit. Options (c) and (d) add or change topic — wrong functions.
